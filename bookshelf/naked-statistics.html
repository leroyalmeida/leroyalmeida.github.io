Naked Statistics: Stripping the Dread from the Data

Author: Charles Wheelan
Year Read: 2020
Summary Statistics can be used to describe and compare, infer, assess risk, and identify important relationships

Key Takeaways:

  • Statistics can be used to describe and compare, infer, assess risk, and identify important relationships.
  • When working with large amounts of data, we simplify and summarize. This involves a loss of detail–which can be good or bad.
    • The most basic task when working with data is to summarize a great deal of information.
    • More data can often present less clarity. So we simplify.
    • Any simplification invites abuse. Descriptive statistics can be like online dating profiles: technically accurate and yet pretty darn misleading.
    • Descriptive statistics exist to simplify, which always implies some loss of nuance or detail.
    • The reality is that you can lie with statistics. Or you can make inadvertent errors.
  • When uncertainty is involved, we want to use probability to manage the risk
    • Any business facing uncertainty can manage these risks by engineering processes so that the probability of an adverse outcome, anything from an environmental catastrophe to a defective product, becomes acceptably low.
  • Scientific method: If we are testing a hypothesis, conduct a controlled experiment in which the variable of interest is the only thing that differs between the experimental group and thee control group. If we observe a marked difference in some outcome between the two groups, we can safely infer that the variable of interest is what caused that outcome.
  • Regression analysis is isolating the relationship between two variables while holding constant the effects of other variables
  • We can say that two variables are correlated but we cannot always say why, and sometimes, we cannot know if one causes the other
    • We can isolate a strong association between two variables by using statistical analysis, but we cannot necessarily explain why that relationship exists, and in some cases, we cannot know for certain that the relationship is causal, meaning that a change in one variable is really causing a change in the other.
  • First descriptive task is usually to find the middle, or the central tendency. This is done by getting the mean, the middle of the distribution, or the median, the point that divides the distribution in half.
    • The most basic measure of the “middle” of a distribution is the mean, or average.
    • The median is the point that divides a distribution in half, meaning that half of the observations lie above the median and half lie below.
    • Quartiles, divides the distribution in quarters. Deciles, in tenths. Percentiles, in hundredths.
      • Each percentile represents 1 percent of the distribution, so that the 1st percentile represents the bottom 1 percent of the distribution and the 99th percentile represents the top 1 percent of the distribution.
  • Absolute vs Relative. Absolute values have meaning in themselves. Relative values only have meaning in comparison to something else.
  • Standard deviation is how dispersed the data is from their mean
  • Data that is normally distributed is symmetrical to their mean, taking the form a bell curve. The proportion of observations within one standard deviation is 68.2%, within two is 95.4%, within three is 99.7%, and so on.
  • Precision vs Accuracy. Precise is how exact we can define something. Accuracy is how consistent it is with the truth.
    • If an answer is accurate, then more precision is usually better. But no amount of precision can make up for inaccuracy.
  • Mean vs Median. The mean is affected by the dispersion of the data. Using mean vs median is dependent on whether the outliers in the distribution distort the message or are part of the message.
  • Statistics is used to describe changes over time
    • Any comparison of a quantity changing over time must have a start point and an end point. One can sometimes manipulate those points in ways that affect the message.
  • Percentages can exaggerate the message
    • Percentages don’t lie—but they can exaggerate. One way to make growth look explosive is to use percentage change to describe some change relative to a very low starting point.
  • Any index is sensitive to how it is constructed. It is affected by what goes into it and how each of those are measured/weighted.
    • A statistical index has all the potential pitfalls of any descriptive statistic—plus the distortions introduced by combining multiple indicators into a single number. By definition, any index is going to be sensitive to how it is constructed; it will be affected both by what measures go into the index and by how each of those measures is weighted.
  • Correlation the degree to which two attributes are related to one another
    • "The degree" == Correlation coefficient
      • Is a single number between –1 to 1.
      • Has no units.
  • Positive vs Negative Correlation. Positively correlated if changing one associates to a change in another in the same direction. Negatively correlated if changing one associates to a change in another in the opposite direction.
  • Correlation does not imply causation
    • Correlation does not imply causation; a positive or negative association between two variables does not necessarily mean that a change in one of the variables is causing the change in the other.
  • Binomial experiment (aka Bernoulli trial) has fixed number of trials, each with two possible outcomes, and the probability of "success" is the same in each trial
  • Probability is the study of events and outcomes involving an element of uncertainty
    • The probability of two independent events happening is the product of their probabilities. The probability of two dependent events happening is the sum of their probabilities.
  • The expected value from an event is the sum of all different outcomes, each weighted by probability and payoff.
  • Law of large numbers says that as the number of trials increases, the average of the outcomes will get closer and closer to the expected value.
  • Unlikely things happen.
    • Unlikely things happen. In fact, over a long enough period of time, they are not even that unlikely.
  • Probability related errors:
    • Assuming events are independent when they're not
      • The likelihood of two independent events’ both happening is the product of their individual probabilities.
    • Not understanding when events are independent
      • Statistical independence is when the outcome of one event has no effect on the outcome of the other.
    • Clusters happen
      • Clusters of cases happen by pure chance, even if it seems improbable, especially when dealing with large numbers.
    • Prosecutor's fallacy
      • The chances of finding a coincidental one in a million match are relatively high if you run the sample through a database with samples from a million people.
    • Reversion to the mean
      • An outlier that is too far from the mean is likely to be followed by outcomes that are more consistent with the long-term average.
    • Statistical discrimination
      • Is it okay to discriminate if the data tell us that we’ll be right far more often than we’re wrong?
  • Inferences from large, properly drawn samples can be as accurate as inferences drawn from the population
  • Best way to draw a sample is to draw them randomly.
    • The easiest way to gather a representative sample of a larger population is to select some subset of that population randomly. (Shockingly, this is known as a simple random sample.)
    • “Gold standard” of research is randomization, a process by which human subjects (or schools, or hospitals, or whatever we’re studying) are randomly assigned to either the treatment or the control group.
    • We do not assume that all the experimental subjects are identical. Instead, probability becomes our friend (once again), and we assume that randomization will evenly divide all relevant characteristics between the two groups—both the characteristics we can observe, like race or income, but also confounding characteristics that we cannot measure or had not considered, such as perseverance or faith.
  • Getting a good sample is difficult
    • Selection Bias
      • How have we chosen the sample or samples that we are evaluating? If each member of the relevant population does not have an equal chance of ending up in the sample, we are going to have a problem with whatever results emerge from that sample.
        • Self-selection bias, will arise whenever individuals volunteer to be in a treatment group. #Biases
          • For example, prisoners who volunteer for a drug treatment group are different from other prisoners because they have volunteered to be in a drug treatment program. If the participants in this program are more likely to stay out of prison after release than other prisoners, that’s great—but it tells us absolutely nothing about the value of the drug treatment program. These former inmates may have changed their lives because the program helped them kick drugs. Or they may have changed their lives because of other factors that also happened to make them more likely to volunteer for a drug treatment program
    • Publication Bias
      • Positive findings are more likely to be published than negative findings, which can skew the results that we see.
    • Recall Bias
      • We have a natural human impulse to understand the present as a logical consequence of things that happened in the past—cause and effect.
    • Survivorship Bias
      • Survivorship bias, which occurs when some or many of the observations are falling out of the sample, changing the composition of the observations that are left and therefore affecting the results of any analysis.
    • Healthy User Bias
      • “At its simplest, the problem is that people who faithfully engage in activities that are good for them—taking a drug as prescribed, for instance, or eating what they believe is a healthy diet—are fundamentally different from those who don’t.”
  • Central limit theorem says that a large, properly drawn sample will resemble the population from which it was drawn.
    • 1. If you draw large, random samples from any population, the means of those samples will be distributed normally around the population mean (regardless of what the distribution of the underlying population looks like).
    • 2. Most sample means will lie reasonably close to the population mean; the standard error is what defines “reasonably close.”
    • 3. The central limit theorem tells us the probability that a sample mean will lie within a certain distance of the population mean. It is relatively unlikely that a sample mean will lie more than two standard errors from the population mean, and extremely unlikely that it will lie three or more standard errors from the population mean.
    • 4. The less likely it is that an outcome has been observed by chance, the more confident we can be in surmising that some other factor is in play.
  • Standard error measures the dispersion of the sample means. Standard error is the standard deviation of the sample means.
  • Sample means will cluster more tightly around the population mean as the sample size gets larger. Sample means will cluster less tightly around the population mean as the population is more spread out.
    • A large sample drawn from a highly dispersed population is also likely to be highly dispersed; a large sample from a population clustered tightly around the mean is also likely to be clustered tightly around the mean.
  • Inference is drawing meaningful conclusions from data. Statistical inference starts with a null hypothesis. If the null hypothesis is rejected, an alternative hypothesis is said to be true.
    • Researchers typically ask, If the null hypothesis is true, how likely is it that we would observe this pattern of data by chance?
  • How implausible does the null hypothesis have to be before we can reject it and invite some alternative explanation?
    • A common threshold is 5% or 0.05. This is also known as a significance level, which is the likelihood of observing some pattern of data if the null hypothesis were true.
  • If the significance level is too low, we incorrectly reject a null hypothesis and get Type I errors or False Positives.
    • Null hypothesis: an individual is not a terrorist. False Positive is arresting someone that is not a terrorist.
  • If the significance level is too high, we fail to reject a null hypothesis that should have been rejected and get Type II errors or False Negatives.
    • Null hypothesis: an individual is not a terrorist. False Negative is letting an individual go when he is a terrorist
  • Standard errors, in polling, tell how much dispersion should be expected from sample to sample. Bigger polls have smaller standard errors. Smaller polls have larger standard errors.
